The revelations

Documents seen by the Guardian show how Luxembourg turns a blind eye to companies avoiding millions in tax in other countries. The companies involved are household names and stalwarts of the FTSE and DowJones. They use highly artificial corporate structures involving subsidiaries and branches in Luxembourg to ...

On paper, Luxembourg is an upstanding member of the club; a founder member of the EU with a corporate tax rate of 29%. But ...

Shire made 69% of its $4.93bn sales of prescription drugs, mainly pills for ADHD and intestinal disease, in the US and Canada last year.

The group’s tax bill for 2013 was $278m, giving an effective tax rate of just 16.4%. That is less than half the headline rate of 35% that most big businesses must pay in the US. It is lower than that of Apple and almost as low as the rate at Google.

One of the main reasons for the Shire’s tax gap is obliquely described in the footnotes to group’s annual report as “intra-group items”. That is, the tax-favourable impact on the group’s tax bills of investments and transactions between Shire group companies around the world.

Despite the enormity of this boost to profits, no more information about fortuitous “intra-group items” ― a regular occurrence for many years ― has been made available. Until now.

Leaked papers showing tax agreements secured from Luxembourg tax officials by PwC, Shire’s tax advisers, reveal how far Shire goes to create these tax-saving internal deals through aggressive and highly artificial tax structures.

In particular, the papers provide the cipher to decode the extraordinary goings-on at one Shire’s internal lending business in Luxembourg, a company known as Shire Holdings Europe No2 Sarl, or SHE2.

Over six years between 2008 and 2013 SHE2 received more than $2bn, almost all of which was declared profit — and taxed at less than 1%. That despite Luxembourg having a headline tax rate of 29%.

Last year alone, SHE2 charged interest of $584m on loans to other Shire companies; loans which by December had risen to $10.1bn. Some of the borrower businesses ― sister companies within the same group ― were charged rates of 8% and 9%, despite Shire’s AAA credit rating?CHECK

The colossal scale of internal lending was equivalent to xx% of the entire value of Shire, and bore no reflection of the group’s overall borrowing position, which in fact showed a net cash surplus of more than $2.2bn.

Despite its manifest importance to the group, however, accounts for SHE2 suggest the company was not well staffed. Spending on employees of less than $51,000 for each of the last six years.

Some colour

When the Guardian called at the group’s offices on Rue Robert Stumper there was little sign of activity. Indeed, the reception staff were initially mystified as to the whereabouts of “Mr Shire”.

Publicly available filings in Luxembourg showed the business had four managers, two of whom included Lawson Rose, Shire’s tax director, and his boss Fearghas Carruthers, head of tax. Neither were available when we called.

The two remaining directors are 66-year-old(check) part-time(check) accountant Leonard de Waal and lawyer Yannick Poos. Poos is also a director of two other Luxembourg businesses, Alter Domus and Business Office Services Sarl, both of which are based out of the same offices on Rue Robert Stumper.

Alter Domus — “Other Home” in Latin — is a company, formerly part of PwC, which provides “professional administration services for multinationals”. On its website, its explains that “Close to 200 multinational corporations, many of them leading names on the most prominent stock exchanges in the world, have entrusted Alter Domus with the administration of their overseas holding and financing companies.”

Business Office Services, which is owned by one of the founders of Alter Domus, offers “high quality, prestigious executive designer offices to an international clientele”. Citing business partners including Alter Domus, it adds: “We are committed to providing not just office space but also an entire suite of business services that are tailored to your specific needs”.

Leaked Luxembourg tax agreements, seen by the Guardian, provide a rare glimpse into the Shire’s complex corporate structures SPIDER DIAGRAM PDF? . Much of the detail is incomplete and unintelligible, but key pieces of information can mapped onto public accounts for Shire companies to unlock the mystery of why SHE2 pays almost no tax.

The answer lies in Dublin, Ireland, where Shire moved its corporate headquarters in 2008? after the Labour had threatened a crackdown on UK multinationals using internal financing companies in aggressive tax planning structures.

Here the group had a holding company called Shire Holdings Ireland No.2 Limited, or SHIL2 for short. A glance at this firm’s accounts, however, gives little away. They show little activity other than the holding of a very large investment in SHE2, which the accounts show it valued at $9bn last year.

But correspondence with the Luxembourg taxman reveals that SHIL2 has been busier than it appears. In fact, a huge internal loan within SHIL2 — not disclosed from the companies accounts — generates a lucrative tax deductions for Shire. This internal loan is between SHIL2 in Dublin and SHIL2’s branch office in Luxembourg. Despite no loans, interest or tax arising in Ireland, however, Luxembourg have awarded a huge tax deduction to SHIL2’s Luxembourg branch.

How it works

Moreover, the taxman in the Grand-Duchy has allowed Shire to use this deduction to off-set the huge profits arising in SHE2.

A search for the address of SHIL2’s Luxembourg branch office throws up a familiar address: it is the same building on Rue Robert Stumper.

When the Guardian went to find out what was going on at the SHIL2 office in Luxembourg, where Shire held $9bn of investments in subsidiaries, once again there was sign of business activity.
